Cultural Factors Domain (All Ages)

These indicators identify linguistic or cultural issues for which service providers need to make accommodations (e.g., provide interpreter, find therapist who speaks family’s primary language, and/or ensure that a child/youth in placement can participate in cultural rituals associated with their cultural identity).

Indicators in the Cultural Factors Domain describe difficulties that children and youth may experience or encounter because of their membership in any cultural group, and/or because of the relationship between members of that group and members of the dominant society.

Health care disparities are differences in health care quality, affordability, access, utilization, and outcomes between groups. Culture in this domain is described broadly to include cultural groups that are racial, ethnic, or religious, or are based on age, sexual orientation, gender identity, socioeconomic status and/or geography.

It is important to remember when using the CANS that the family should be defined from the individual child/youth’s perspective (i.e., who the child/youth describes as part of their family). The cultural issues in this domain should be considered in relation to the impact they are having on the life of the child/youth when rating these indicators and creating a treatment or service plan.

The indicators in this domain are rated for all ages. For ages 0–5, rate these indicators from the perspective of the family.

Question to Consider

How does the child/youth’s and/or their family’s membership in a particular cultural group impact their stress and well-being?

Action Levels

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring, watchful waiting, or preventive action based on history, suspicion or disagreement.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. Action is required to ensure that the identified need is addressed.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling; requires immediate and/or intensive action.

Language and/or Literacy

This indicator looks at whether the child/youth and family need help with communication to obtain the necessary resources, supports and accommodations (e.g., interpreter). This includes spoken, written and sign language as well as issues of literacy.

Questions to Consider

  • What language does the family speak at home?
  • Is there a child/youth interpreting for the family in situations that may compromise the child/youth or family’s care?
  • Does the child/youth or significant family members have any special needs related to communication (e.g., ESL, ASL, Braille, or assisted technology)?

Ratings and Descriptions

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action. No evidence that there is a need or preference for an interpreter and/or the child/youth and family speak and read the primary language where they live.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring, watchful waiting, or preventive action based on history, suspicion or disagreement. Child/youth and/or family speak or read the primary language where they live, but potential communication problems exist because of limited vocabulary or comprehension of the nuances of the language.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. Action is required to ensure that the identified need is addressed. Child/youth and/or significant family members do not speak the primary language where they live. Translator or family’s native language speaker is needed for successful intervention; a qualified individual(s) can be identified within natural supports.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling; requires immediate and/or intensive action. Child/youth and/or significant family members do not speak the primary language where they live. Translator or family’s native language speaker is needed for successful intervention; no such individual is available from among natural supports.

Traditions and Cultural Rituals

This indicator rates the child/youth and family’s access to and participation in cultural traditions, rituals and practices, including the celebration of culturally specific holidays such as Kwanzaa, Dia de los Muertos, Yom Kippur, Quinceañera, etc. This may also include daily activities that are culturally specific (e.g., wearing a hijab, praying toward Mecca at specific times, eating a specific diet, access to media), and traditions and activities to include newer cultural identities.

Questions to Consider

  • What traditions and customs does the child/youth and family practice or participate in?
  • Does the child/youth or family fear discrimination for practicing their cultural traditions or rituals?

Ratings and Descriptions

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action. The child/youth and/or family is consistently able to practice traditions and rituals consistent with their cultural identity.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring, watchful waiting, or preventive action. The child/youth and/or family is generally able to practice traditions and rituals consistent with their cultural identity; however, they sometimes experience some obstacles.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. The child/youth and/or family experiences significant barriers and is sometimes prevented from practicing traditions and rituals consistent with their cultural identity.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling. The child/youth and/or family is unable to practice traditions and rituals consistent with their cultural identity.

Cultural Stress

This indicator identifies circumstances in which the child/youth and/or family’s cultural identity is met with hostility or other problems within their environment due to differences in attitudes, behavior, or beliefs of others. This includes cultural differences that are causing stress between the child/youth and their family. Racism, negativity toward SOGIE, and other forms of discrimination would be rated here.

Questions to Consider

  • Has the child/youth and/or family ever been treated poorly because of their beliefs, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, etc.?
  • What does the family believe is the reality of their discrimination? Does this impact their functioning?
  • How does the caregiver support the child/youth’s identity if different from their own?

Ratings and Descriptions

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action. No evidence of stress between the child/youth and/or family’s cultural identity and their environment.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring. Some occasional stress resulting from friction between the child/youth and/or family’s cultural identity and their environment.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. Cultural stress is causing problems in at least one life domain. Support is needed to manage it.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling. High level of cultural stress is making functioning difficult. Immediate planning is needed to reduce stress.

Cultural Congruence

This indicator refers to a family’s child-rearing practices, understanding of child development, and early intervention in comparison to the prevailing professional/helping culture(s) perspective.

Questions to Consider

  • How does the family’s culture impact their child-rearing practices?
  • Are there cultural differences in the caregiver’s practices that differ from the majority culture?

Ratings and Descriptions

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action. No cultural differences considered problematic for the child/youth.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring. Some cultural differences exist but are not considered risky.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. Cultural differences are considered problematic for the child/youth.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling. Cultural differences are considered abusive or neglectful and may result in intervention.

Cultural Differences Within the Family

This indicator addresses differences in background, values, or perspectives among family members. This may occur in families with adopted children from different cultural backgrounds or in immigrant families where children may prefer U.S. culture over traditional practices.

Questions to Consider

  • Do the caregiver and child/youth have different understandings of appropriate behaviors rooted in cultural traditions?
  • Do they understand and respect each other’s perspectives?
  • Do these differences cause conflict that impacts the child/youth’s functioning?

Ratings and Descriptions

  • 0 – No evidence of any needs; no need for action. No conflict or stress due to cultural differences.
  • 1 – Identified need that requires monitoring. Some past struggles, but currently managed well.
  • 2 – Need is interfering with functioning. Cultural differences are negatively impacting the child/youth’s functioning.
  • 3 – Need is dangerous or disabling. Cultural differences are significantly interfering with functioning and require immediate action.
